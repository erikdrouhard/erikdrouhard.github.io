NUANCE · 2017–2019

Dragon Drive

Designing a gaze-driven windshield HUD that made an intelligent car’s behavior legible without asking drivers to leave the road.

  • TECHNICAL DESIGNER
  • UI + INTERACTION LEAD
  • GAZE-DRIVEN HUD
  • EMBEDDED MOTION

01 · THE CHALLENGE

Designing for a glance, not a screen

A windshield interface sits inside the driver’s field of view. It cannot demand the same attention as a phone or dashboard app. Information has to arrive at the right moment, reveal just enough detail, and make every state change understandable while the road remains primary.

Dragon Drive brought voice, gaze, a projected HUD, and an embedded center display into one real-time experience. I led the UI and interaction design for the windshield HUD and worked as the technical designer translating prototypes and animation intent into behavior the display systems could carry.

Environment
Moving vehicle, changing light, visually noisy streets.
Attention
Divided, time-sensitive, and anchored on the road.
System
Windshield HUD, gaze input, voice, and embedded display.
Design job
Make focus, response, and system state readable at a glance.

02 · INTERACTION MODEL

Gaze provided intent. Motion provided confidence.

The HUD needed to acknowledge attention without turning every look into a disruptive action. A stable overview preserved orientation; focus and motion then made the system’s response visible.

01

Orient

Keep the primary domains stable and spatially predictable so the interface can be read peripherally.

02

Focus

Apply gaze as selection context, making the attended region clear before revealing more information.

03

Respond

Use motion and multimodal feedback to show what changed, what is active, and where detail moved.

DRIVER Attention Eyes remain oriented toward the road.
→
HUD Focus The attended tile acknowledges gaze.
→
SYSTEM Response Motion and the embedded display carry detail.

The interface was not a second dashboard floating on the windshield. It was a compact layer for orientation and action, paired with a richer display when the task needed more detail.

03 · MY ROLE

I translated design intent into embedded behavior

As the technical designer, I worked between interaction design and implementation. I used prototypes and animation studies to make the intended behavior concrete, then translated that behavior for the embedded display and HUD teams.

The work was not simply making transitions look polished. Timing, hierarchy, movement, and feedback each carried meaning. The handoff had to preserve that meaning as the experience moved from a design prototype into a constrained, real-time automotive system.

  1. 01 · PROTOTYPE

    Make the moment tangible

    Model gaze, focus, selection, and feedback as a complete interaction rather than a sequence of static screens.

  2. 02 · DECOMPOSE

    Name the states and transitions

    Break the prototype into triggers, state changes, timing, layering, and visual assets that could be discussed precisely.

  3. 03 · TRANSLATE

    Adapt motion to the display system

    Shape the behavior for the embedded pipeline and HUD while protecting the interaction’s hierarchy and intent.

  4. 04 · VALIDATE

    Evaluate it in physical context

    Review the experience against road noise, divided attention, projection, and the behavior of the full in-car system.

THE DESIGN-ENGINEERING BRIDGE

The prototype became a shared language

Product and interaction intent could be experienced early; implementation partners could inspect the behavior they needed to reproduce. That bridge let motion function as a specification, not decoration.

04 · THE SHOWCASE

A working system in a moving vehicle

The HUD was featured at the Dragon Drive Innovation Showcase at CES 2019. The in-vehicle demonstration made the complete interaction visible: gaze on the windshield layer, animated feedback, voice, and the embedded display behaving as one system.

OUTCOME

The project made an intelligent system’s focus and response visible where software meets the physical world.

My contribution was UI and interaction-design leadership plus technical translation for the HUD and embedded experience. The underlying gaze technology, vehicle platform, and wider Dragon Drive engineering were collaborative system inputs—not my individual inventions.
